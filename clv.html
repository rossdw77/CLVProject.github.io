01 — Problem Statement

What are customers actually worth?

CLV prediction matters because marketing resources are finite and customer value is not evenly distributed. A business needs to know which customers drive disproportionate revenue so retention efforts are concentrated where they have the most impact. At the same time, low and mid-tier customers represent the majority of the population, and understanding their behavior ensures they are not overlooked in favor of chasing a small high-value segment. By accurately predicting where each customer sits in the value distribution, marketing teams can tailor both outreach frequency and product targeting to match customer profile. This is where interpretability becomes operational. SHAP analysis of this model surfaces electronics affinity as a dominant driver of high-value customer predictions, giving a marketing team a concrete signal about which product categories to lead with for their most valuable segment.

Key question: Given a customer's behavior in their first N days, how much will they spend over the following 12 months?

Binning Strategy

The data is segmented into spend bins prior to modeling. This is a deliberate architectural choice to handle the dispersity of spend distributions across customer value tiers. A single model fit to the full population would underperform in both tails.

05 — Modeling

Separate models, deliberate architecture.

Each spend bin gets its own XGBRegressor, stored in a dictionary keyed by bin name. This per-segment approach lets each model learn the dynamics specific to that value tier without being regularized toward the population mean.

SHAP bridges model outputs to decisions.
