01 — Problem Statement

What are customers actually worth?

CLV prediction matters because marketing resources are finite and customer value is not evenly distributed. A business needs to know which customers drive disproportionate revenue so retention efforts are concentrated where they have the most impact. At the same time, low and mid-tier customers represent the majority of the population, and understanding their behavior ensures they are not overlooked in favor of chasing a small high-value segment.

By accurately predicting where each customer sits in the value distribution, marketing teams can tailor both outreach frequency and product targeting to match customer profile. This is where interpretability becomes operational. SHAP analysis of this model surfaces electronics affinity as a dominant driver of high-value customer predictions, giving a marketing team a concrete signal about which product categories to lead with for their most valuable segment.

Target variable: Total_Amount spent per customer, aggregated from transaction-level data across a 15-month observation window.

Key Constraints

  • Short observation window: 15 months of transaction data limited how much purchasing behavior each model could be trained on.
  • Persistent skew after cleaning: Even after outlier removal, spend variability was more extreme than typical customer transaction datasets, making a single global model structurally inadequate.
  • Low purchase frequency: Median frequency of 2 transactions per customer constrained the ability of probabilistic models to separately identify purchase and dropout processes.

03 — Workflow

End-to-end coding infrastructure.

The project is organized as a modular Python pipeline where each file handles a distinct stage of the process. This separation keeps configuration, data, modeling, and evaluation concerns independent and makes the pipeline easy to extend or re-run in parts.

Pipeline Modules

config
   Sets model parameters: file paths, column identifiers,
   train/test date split, and spend bin boundaries.

data_loader
   Loads the raw CSV transaction file and applies
   outlier thresholds defined in config.

visualizations
   Generates EDA charts: spend distribution analysis,
   categorical slices by product category, and time
   series review of transaction volume.

preprocessing
   Aggregates transaction-level data to customer level
   and engineers new feature columns including RFM
   variables and category affinity metrics.

model_prep
   Builds train/test splits for each modeling approach
   based on the dependent variable. Handles separate
   splits for regression, classification, and
   statistical modeling workflows.

classification_models
   Trains and evaluates Logistic Regression, Random
   Forest, and XGBoost classifiers on CLV tier labels
   (low / mid / high).

regression_models
   Trains and evaluates Linear Regression, Random
   Forest, and XGBoost regressors on continuous
   Total_Amount targets, both globally and per bin.

statistical_models
   Implements BG/NBD (BTYD) and Gamma-Gamma models
   for probabilistic CLV estimation via pymc-marketing.

evaluation
   Produces regression results (MAPE per bin, decile
   metrics), classifier results (ROC/AUC, confusion
   matrix), SHAP plots, and XGBoost tree visualizations.

04 — Data

Feature design is where the leverage is.

The dataset contains 15 months of e-commerce transaction records aggregated to the customer level. Raw transactions are pivoted and merged to produce a single row per customer with behavioral and categorical features as columns. The target variable is Total_Amount: the sum of all spend across the observation window.

Feature Groups

  • RFM Features: Recency (days since last purchase), Frequency (transaction count), and Monetary value (total spend) computed across the full observation window.
  • Category Affinity: Transaction counts by product category including Electronics, Fashion, Home and Garden, Sports, Books, Beauty, Toys, and Food. These capture what each customer buys, not just how much.
  • Behavioral Metrics: Units per transaction (UpT) and spend per transaction (DpT), capturing average basket composition.
  • Customer Attributes: Age, tenure, session duration, pages viewed, and delivery time. These were included in the feature set but contributed lower importance scores relative to RFM and category variables.

05 — Modeling

Separate models, deliberate architecture.

The starting hypothesis was straightforward: a regression model trained on RFM features and customer attributes should be sufficient to predict CLV. It was not.

The spend distribution spans two orders of magnitude with heavy right skew, and a single regression model forced to fit that entire range allocated most of its predictive capacity to high-spend customers. Performance on low and mid-tier customers, who make up the majority of the population, was poor.

Classification reframed the problem as segment assignment rather than value prediction, and the XGBoost classifier performed well on high and low tiers. Mid-tier separation was weaker, which is expected given the distributional overlap at those boundaries. But classification trades away the precision needed for budget allocation decisions. Knowing a customer is high-tier is less actionable than knowing they are likely worth 8,000 TRY.

The multi-staged approach attempted to combine both: classify first, then regress within each predicted segment. Train MAPE was acceptable but test MAPE degraded significantly across all bins. The classification step introduced label noise at segment boundaries, and the downstream regression models overfit to mislabeled populations rather than learning the true spend signal.

The binned regression architecture resolved this by removing the classification dependency entirely. Spend bins were defined on true decile values, separate regression models were trained within each bin, and each model only needed to explain variance within a narrow spend range. Test MAPE landed at 20-25% across bins.

Hyperparameter Tuning

Hyperparameter search used randomized cross-validation across both XGBoost and Random Forest models. Given the right-skewed spend distribution and relatively limited dataset size, the search grid was deliberately biased toward regularization from the start.

max_depth was capped at 5 and the best result landed at 4, sitting cleanly in the middle of the grid. min_child_weight was kept high at 20 to ensure sufficient observations at each leaf and limit spurious splits on low-frequency customers. reg_lambda was set well above the XGBoost default across the entire grid, reflecting a prior expectation that the model would need explicit penalization to generalize across a skewed target. The selected learning rate of 0.01 paired with 500 estimators favored slow, stable convergence over speed. 

param_grid = {
    "n_estimators":     [500, 1000, 1500],

06 — Interpretability

SHAP bridges model outputs to decisions.

Predictive accuracy alone does not make a model useful. SHAP values expose why the model assigns a given CLV estimate, which is what enables business action. A TreeExplainer was instantiated per bin, producing a consistent interpretability layer across the full segmented architecture.

The XGBoost tree below shows the first split is on Electronics. This is not incidental. Electronics affinity is the single most discriminating variable at the root level, meaning it is the feature the model reaches for first when partitioning the customer population by expected spend.

XGBoost Tree #0 — Electronics as the primary split node

The waterfall plot below shows what happens when that signal is absent. This customer has zero electronics purchases and a quantity of 1. The model's population average prediction is 2,653 TRY, but electronics absence contributes a negative SHAP value of 863 and low quantity contributes a further 1,191 downward, collapsing the final prediction to 60 TRY. The model is not guessing; it is making a legible, auditable decision.

07 — Conclusion

What this work demonstrates.

CLV prediction is a solved problem in theory and a messy one in practice. This project reflects how I actually work: start with a clear hypothesis, measure honestly against it, and adapt when the data tells you something the original plan did not account for.

Flat regression failed not because of poor execution but because a single model cannot adequately capture a customer population that spans two orders of magnitude in spend. The classification approach reframed the problem usefully but sacrificed the precision that makes CLV actionable for budget allocation. The binned regression architecture resolved both constraints, with separate models per spend segment each calibrated to the variance profile of that population, achieving 20-25% test MAPE across all bins.

The real output of this project is not a model. It is a repeatable framework for navigating the gap between what is technically optimal and what is operationally viable. Every modeling decision involves a tradeoff between accuracy and maintainability, or precision and interpretability. Surfacing those tradeoffs clearly is what separates analysis from a business recommendation.

If I Were to Extend This

  • Probabilistic CLV bounds: Add uncertainty estimates per bin so predictions come with confidence ranges, making them more defensible in budget discussions.
  • Segment migration tracking: Extend the framework to flag customers whose behavioral features are shifting toward a higher spend bin over time.
  • Marketing activation layer: Connect SHAP feature outputs directly to campaign targeting logic, so the interpretability work feeds downstream decisions automatically.

In the age of AI, the differentiator is not access to models. It is the judgment to know when a model is misleading you and the architecture to fix it.
